Influence of Temperature on the Growth of Common Bean Plants (Phaseolus vulgaris L.)

Author

Corresponding Author

Published

May 5, 2026

Abstract

Temperature regulates essential physiological processes in common bean, including germination, leaf expansion, photosynthesis, and biomass allocation. This manuscript evaluates the effect of four constant thermal regimes on the early development of common bean plants (Phaseolus vulgaris L.) grown under greenhouse conditions. Temperatures of 15°C, 20°C, 25°C, and 30°C were compared for 30 days after sowing, with 5 replicates per treatment. Plant height, leaf number, leaf area, and shoot dry mass were measured as growth indicators.

The results showed a super significant plant response to the temperature gradient. The 25°C treatment promoted the strongest vegetative growth, whereas 15°C reduced biomass accumulation and delayed leaf emergence. The observed response suggests that moderate temperatures support coordination between leaf expansion and assimilate production, while low temperatures restrict metabolism and delay the formation of photosynthetically active leaf area. These findings reinforce the importance of thermal management and sowing-date planning in production systems exposed to climatic variation.

Keywords: plant physiology; early growth; thermal stress; leaf area; crop management.

Introduction

Temperature is one of the most important abiotic factors controlling plant growth and development. It affects the rate of metabolic reactions, enzyme activity, water and nutrient uptake, cell expansion, and net photosynthesis. In annual crops, small thermal differences during the early stages can alter stand establishment, crop-cycle duration, and the plant’s ability to intercept radiation throughout development.

Common bean (Phaseolus vulgaris L.) is a major food crop in tropical and subtropical regions. In addition to its economic importance, it has strong social relevance because it is part of the basic diet of many populations. However, crop productivity is often limited by unfavorable environmental conditions, including temperatures below or above the optimal range for germination and vegetative growth.

During emergence and the first weeks of development, common bean plants are highly sensitive to thermal variation. Low temperatures tend to reduce emergence speed, limit leaf expansion, and extend the time required to form an efficient canopy. High temperatures, in contrast, may increase evaporative demand, intensify reserve consumption, and disrupt the balance between vegetative growth and metabolic maintenance.

Although the suitable temperature range for common bean is widely discussed in technical recommendations, it remains useful to experimentally demonstrate how different temperatures affect simple growth components. Controlled trials make it possible to isolate thermal effects from other field factors, such as water availability, soil fertility, incoming radiation, and pest pressure. Therefore, this study aimed to evaluate the early growth of common bean plants submitted to different temperature regimes under protected cultivation.

Materials and Methods

The experiment was carried out in a greenhouse with controlled temperature. A completely randomized design was used with four thermal treatments: 15°C, 20°C, 25°C, and 30°C. Each treatment included 5 replicates, totaling 20 experimental units. Each experimental unit consisted of a plastic pot filled with sterilized commercial substrate and two plants after thinning.

Seeds were visually selected for integrity and size uniformity. Sowing was performed at a depth of 3 centimeters, and irrigation was maintained close to field capacity throughout the experimental period. After emergence, thinning was performed to standardize plant density per pot. The experiment was monitored daily for 30 days after sowing.

Hence, biometric evaluations were performed weekly. Plant height was measured from the collar to the apex of the main stem using a graduated ruler. Leaf number was recorded by counting fully expanded leaves. Leaf area was estimated from digital images obtained under standardized lighting and processed with image-analysis software. At the end of the experiment, shoots were harvested, placed in paper bags, and dried in an oven until constant mass.

Data were submitted to analysis of variance. When treatment effects were significant, means were compared using Tukey’s test at the 5% probability level. Regression models were also fitted to describe the response of each variable to the temperature gradient. Analyses were performed in R, with each pot considered an experimental unit.

Results

Temperature significantly affected all biometric variables. The greatest plant-height growth was observed at 25°C, with an average increase of 2.3 cm per day between the second and fourth weeks. At 15°C, the average increase was only 0.8 cm per day, indicating strong limitation of vegetative growth under cold conditions. The 20°C treatment showed an intermediate response, whereas 30°C maintained high growth but remained below the level observed at 25°C.

Leaf number also increased with temperature up to 25°C. Plants maintained at 15°C showed lower leaf emission and a visible delay in the expansion of the first trifoliate leaves. At 25°C, plants displayed greater uniformity among replicates and a more vigorous vegetative architecture. At 30°C, although leaf emission remained relatively high, some plants showed smaller leaves and early signs of reduced turgor during the warmest hours of the day.

Leaf area followed a quadratic response to temperature, with maximum values estimated between 23°C and 27°C. Plants grown at 15°C had leaf area 62% lower than plants grown in the optimal range. This result shows that temperature affects not only leaf number but also the expansion capacity of each leaf blade. Because leaf area determines light interception, its reduction tends to limit later biomass accumulation.

Shoot dry mass followed a trend similar to leaf area. The highest accumulation was recorded at 25°C, while the lowest value occurred at 15°C. The 30°C treatment did not sharply reduce dry mass, but it showed greater variability among replicates. This variability suggests that higher temperatures may amplify individual differences in vigor or small differences in water availability, even under controlled conditions.

Discussion

The results confirm that early growth of common bean strongly depends on the thermal regime. The superior response observed at 25°C is consistent with the temperature range usually associated with vegetative growth in this crop. Under this condition, leaf expansion, new leaf emission, and dry-mass accumulation occurred in a coordinated manner, indicating a favorable balance between assimilate production and use.

The growth reduction at 15°C can be attributed to lower enzyme activity and slower processes related to cell division and expansion. Under low temperatures, plants tend to show lower relative growth rate, delayed phenological development, and reduced capacity to rapidly form an active canopy. This behavior is especially relevant in regions where sowing occurs during seasonal transitions, when cold nights can limit emergence and early establishment.

The performance observed at 30°C indicates that high temperatures do not always cause immediate growth reduction, but they may narrow the plant’s physiological safety margin. Under this condition, small water deficits or additional temperature increases can intensify transpiration losses and reduce photosynthetic efficiency. Therefore, agronomic interpretation should consider not only mean temperature but also daily thermal amplitude, relative humidity, and water availability in the substrate.

From a practical perspective, the results reinforce the need for careful sowing-date planning. In areas exposed to low temperatures at the beginning of the cycle, delayed sowing or the use of more adapted cultivars may reduce establishment risks. In warm regions, strategies such as adequate irrigation, soil cover, and the selection of periods with lower thermal stress can help maintain vegetative growth within a favorable range.

Conclusion

Temperature strongly influenced the early growth of common bean plants. The 25°C treatment resulted in greater height, higher leaf number, larger leaf area, and greater shoot dry-mass accumulation. The 15°C treatment limited vegetative development, while 30°C maintained relatively high growth but with greater variability among plants. The results indicate that temperatures near 25°C favor early establishment of common bean and should be considered when planning experiments, protected cultivation, and sowing calendars.

Limitations and Future Work

This study was conducted under controlled conditions and evaluated only early growth. Under field conditions, plant response may be modified by solar radiation, soil moisture, fertility, pathogens, and daily thermal fluctuations. Future studies should investigate the interaction between temperature and water availability, in addition to comparing cultivars with different levels of tolerance to thermal stress. It is also recommended to monitor the full crop cycle to verify whether early growth differences are reflected in grain yield.
